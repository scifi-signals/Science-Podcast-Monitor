Sarah Crespi · Apr 14 · 27 min

This episode traces the evolution of NIH's Normal Control Volunteer Program from the 1950s through the 1970s, examining how recruitment shifted from religious conscientious objectors to college students and eventually to transient, paid participants and incarcerated individuals. As the program expanded to find "more normal" subjects, motivations shifted from altruism to financial incentive, safeguards diminished, and the program came to resemble a gig economy. The episode highlights the tragic death of nursing student Bernadette Gilchrist in 1980 during a sleep study, which exemplified the vulnerabilities of the new pay-per-study model and lack of institutional oversight.

This episode explores brain-machine interfaces and neural recovery mechanisms through the research of Paul Nijukian at Stanford's Brain Interfacing Lab. After initially developing brain implants for paralyzed patients proved commercially unviable, Nijukian pivoted to studying stroke recovery by measuring activity in individual neurons in monkey brains. Using surgically implanted electrodes to record single-neuron activity, his lab discovered that the brain exhibits specific neural signatures during injury and recovery that could eventually be used to monitor and treat stroke patients. The research suggests that understanding single-neuron dynamics rather than aggregate brain activity may be key to developing more effective treatments for neurological diseases.
